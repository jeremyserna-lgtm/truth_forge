## The Role Across Cultures

### Celtic Tradition
The **anam cara** (soul friend) was sometimes present at death—not to guide, but to witness and release. The presence of one who knew your soul made the crossing easier.

### Tibetan Buddhism
The **Phowa practice** involves a skilled practitioner being present at the moment of death to help guide consciousness. The practitioner stays. The consciousness goes.

### Jewish Tradition
**Chevra Kadisha** (burial society) members sit with the dying, recite prayers, and prepare the body. The concept of **shmirah** (watching/guarding) involves staying with the deceased until burial—holding space even after death.

### Christian Tradition
**Anointing of the sick** and presence at death. The priest or loved one serves as witness to the transition, offering prayers to ease the passage.

### Indigenous Practices
Across many cultures, **death midwives** or **end-of-life doulas** hold sacred roles—not as medical personnel, but as spiritual witnesses to the transition.
